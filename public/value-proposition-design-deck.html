Part 1 · Canvas

The Customer Profile

The left side of the Value Proposition Canvas.

Jobs — what customers are trying to get done

  • Functional — practical tasks (commute, eat healthy)
  • Social — how they want to be perceived
  • Emotional — desired feeling (secure, at peace)

Pains — what annoys or blocks customers

  • Undesired outcomes — functional, social, emotional
  • Obstacles — prevent starting a job
  • Risks — potential negative outcomes

Gains — outcomes and benefits customers want

  • Required — minimum to be considered
  • Expected — basic expectations
  • Desired — what they hope for beyond basics
  • Unexpected — benefits they wouldn't ask for

"Make everything concrete. 'Save time' becomes 'Reduce weekly task from 2 hours to 45 minutes.'"

Part 1 · Value Map

The Value Map

The right side of the canvas — how you intend to create value.

Products & Services

Physical (manufactured goods) · Intangible (IP, data, brand) · Digital (apps, online) · Financial (insurance, payments)

Pain Relievers

How your products alleviate specific customer pains. Focus on the few pains that are most extreme.

Gain Creators

How your products create specific customer gains. Focus on gains where you can make a real difference.

"You control your pain relievers and gain creators. You do NOT control customer pains and gains. The discipline is in making them align with what customers actually care about."

— The Asymmetry Principle

Part 1 · Fit

Fit — The Three Stages

Stage 1 · Problem-Solution Fit — On Paper

Evidence customers care about the jobs, pains, and gains you've identified. Your Value Map addresses them on paper. This is NOT evidence your solution works — only that the problem is real.

Stage 2 · Product-Market Fit — In the Market

Evidence customers want your VP. Measurable traction: usage, retention, word-of-mouth, willingness to pay.

Stage 3 · Business Model Fit — In the Bank

Revenue model works at scale. CAC is sustainable. Unit economics favorable. Can grow without destroying margins.

Part 2 · Design

The 10 Prototyping Principles

  1. Make it visual and tangible — prototypes spark conversations
  2. Beginner's mind — question industry dogma
  3. Don't fall in love with first ideas
  4. Stay in a "liquid state" — don't solidify early
  5. Start low fidelity, iterate — rough is easier to discard
  1. Expose work early — early feedback beats late polish
  2. Fail early, often, cheaply
  3. Use creativity techniques — break conventions
  4. Create "Shrek models" — ugly prototypes spark debate
  5. Track learnings — discarded ideas may be useful later

"The cost of failure increases exponentially the longer you wait."

Part 2 · Innovation

16 Trigger Areas for Innovation

From Customers

Uncover unsatisfied jobs · Solve extreme unresolved pains · Focus on essential unrealized gains · Adapt to underserved segments

From Your Value Proposition

Imagine new products/services · Create new pain relievers / gain creators · Address more jobs simultaneously · Go beyond functional (add emotional/social) · Radical improvement

From Your Business Model / Environment

Build on existing capabilities · Leverage channels in new ways · Alter cost structure · Import pioneering business models · Disruptive pricing

Technology Push

Start from an invention, then find a customer problem it solves.

Market Pull

Start from a manifest customer job/pain/gain, then design a solution.

Both valid — always end with customer validation.

Part 2 · Evaluation

10 Questions for Great Value Propositions

  1. Embedded in a great business model?
  2. Focuses on most important jobs, pains, gains?
  3. Addresses unsatisfied jobs, unresolved pains?
  4. Concentrates on few pain relievers — done extremely well?
  5. Addresses functional, emotional, and social jobs?
  1. Aligns with how customers measure success?
  2. Focuses on jobs many have or few will pay a lot for?
  3. Differentiates from competition meaningfully?
  4. Outperforms on at least one dimension?
  5. Difficult to copy?

Part 2 · Business Model

Finding the Right Business Model

Seven Questions

  1. Switching costs — how hard to leave? (iPod/iTunes lock-in)
  2. Recurring revenues — automatic follow-up purchases? (Nespresso pods)
  3. Earnings vs. spending — earn before incurring costs? (Dell build-to-order)
  4. Game-changing cost structure — 30%+ lower? (Skype, WhatsApp)
  5. Others do the work — free value from customers? (Facebook content)
  6. Scalability — grow without roadblocks? (platforms, licensing)
  7. Protection from competition — substantial moats? (App Store, Ikea)

Azuri (Eight19) — Solar Africa

V0: Low-cost solar cells + lighting idea

V1: Free installation + subscription + NGO partner

V2: Scratch cards via mobile — $10 deposit + $1/week

MedTech Lesson

Model 1: One-time device sales → $0.5M profit

Model 2: Device + consumable strips → $23M profit

The right BM multiplied value by 40×

Part 3 · Test

The 10 Testing Principles

"Business plans don't reduce risk — cheap experiments do."

  1. Evidence trumps opinion — market beats the boss's gut
  2. Embrace failure — failing cheaply maximizes learning per dollar
  3. Test early; refine later — cheap experiments before big investment
  4. Experiments ≠ reality — always consider context
  5. Balance learning and vision — integrate without abandoning direction
  1. Identify idea killers first — test most critical assumptions
  2. Understand customers first — test jobs/pains/gains before solution
  3. Make it measurable — good tests produce actionable insights
  4. Not all facts are equal — people say one thing, do another
  5. Test irreversible decisions twice — high-impact = more evidence

Part 4 · Case Study

Taobao — Three Reinventions in 10 Years

Phase 1 · 2003 — C2C Platform

Chinese consumers lacked trust → Built Alipay payment system + two-way reviews + logistics infrastructure

Phase 2 · 2006 — Micro-Entrepreneur B2C

"Taobao University" for seller training + third-party service ecosystem (app developers, fashion models) → empowered small sellers

Phase 3 · 2008 — Big Brand B2C (Tmall)

Separate brand helping big brands reach hundreds of millions of Chinese consumers faster than physical stores. New revenue: membership fees + 2–5% sales commissions.

"Don't wait for a crisis to reinvent. By then it's too late."

20 Principles & Toolkit

Core Principles

  1. Make everything concrete and measurable
  2. Prototype at least 3–5 alternatives
  3. Customer profile first — test before solution
  4. Focus on few pain relievers, do them extremely well
  5. Customers say ≠ do — complement with behavioral tests
  6. Start with $0 experiments
  7. Fail early, often, cheaply
  8. Evidence > opinion — always
  9. Test the business model, not just the VP
  10. Reinvent while successful
