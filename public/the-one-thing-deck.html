01 / 17
Core idea

Extraordinary results are not produced by broad effort.

They come from narrowing. Ignore everything you could do. Find the one thing you should do. Give it disproportionate time.

The book calls this Going Small: choosing the smallest action with the largest future consequence.

02 / 17
Going small

The strategic contrast of focus

Big success

Subtract distractions. Master a vital few things. Align the day around the ultimate objective.

Small life

Overload the calendar. Chase low-value tasks. Create execution gaps and constant friction.

Busyness is not leverage. Focus is leverage.

05 / 17
Mastery loop

The virtuous cycle

1. Passion

Interest pulls attention toward one domain.

2. Time

Disproportionate time compounds faster than scattered effort.

3. Skill

Focused reps create specialized capability.

4. Results

Results increase passion, restarting the loop.

The enemy is not lack of ambition. It is misallocated attention.

07 / 17
Lie 01

Everything does not matter equally.

A to-do list is a survival list. A success list is a force-ranked list of what actually moves the goal.

Whittle the 20% down to the vital few of the vital few.

The lead domino is rarely hidden by complexity. It is hidden by equality.

08 / 17
Lie 02

Multitasking is task switching.

Every switch carries reorientation cost: slower work, weaker memory, more loose ends.

Dopamine loop

Switching feels exciting, so distraction starts to feel like progress.

Cognitive load

The brain cannot deeply attend to two demanding things at once.

Time loss

Interruptions can waste a third of the day through recovery time.

09 / 17
Lie 03

You do not need a disciplined life.

You need selected discipline long enough to build the right habit.

66

Day sweet spot

Average time for a behavior to become automatic.

1

Habit at a time

Concentrate discipline until automation takes over.

10 / 17
Lie 04

Willpower is not always on call.

Willpower behaves like a fast-twitch muscle. It renews, but it drains quickly.

High fuel

Better restraint, better judgment, harder choices possible.

Low fuel

The brain defaults to the easiest answer — often "no," delay, or avoidance.

Do your ONE Thing early, before your willpower account is empty.

11 / 17
Lie 05

Balance is the wrong target.

Work: go long

Extraordinary results require deliberate imbalance long enough to build mastery.

Life: go short

Health, family, friends, and integrity are glass balls. Do not drop them for long.

Counterbalance. Do not worship balance.

12 / 17
Lie 06

Big is not bad.

The fear of big creates shrinking thinking. If the mountain scares you, you never build mountain-climbing skills.

Think big. Then go small.

Big defines the direction. Small defines the next strike.

13 / 17
Part 2

The Focusing Question

What's the ONE Thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?

This is not motivational copy. It is a prioritization machine.

14 / 17
Question anatomy

Why the question works

1

"The ONE Thing" forces you past what is merely doable.

2

"Such that by doing it" links immediate action to a concrete result.

3

"Everything else easier or unnecessary" identifies leverage, not activity.

Operating rule

Your daily lead domino

Find it. Protect it. Knock it down before the day gets noisy.
